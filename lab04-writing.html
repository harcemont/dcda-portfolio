Lab 4 Reflection

In Lab 4, I used Tableau to build and publish visualizations using a real dataset. The first part of this lab was honestly one of the hardest parts because I have to find a dataset that Tableau could actually handle in a way that wouldn’t turn into a mess. I tested a few datasets first and kept running into problems like missing columns, weird formatting, or data that was either too broad to make a visualizaton or too detailed for Tableau to group in a meaningful way. After some trial and error, I landed on the Bad Drivers dataset because it had clear variables and it uploaded cleanly, which made it way easier to start building visuals that actually made sense.

Once I had the dataset loaded, my main goal was to determine how I could make this data understandable without overwhelming people. Since the data is state-based, I thought the clearest thing to visualize was would be ranking them by showing which states show up highest for certain crash-related factors. That’s why I built multiple visualizations around top 10 bar charts, each focusing on a different factor related to fatal collisions. I went with bar charts because they’re simple, familiar, and good for comparison. I also filtered to only show the top 10 states for each category because showing all 50 would’ve been overwhelming. Using top 10 kept the focus on the biggest cases and made it easier to compare patterns between the charts.

I also tried to keep the design consistent across everything to make the visuals feel easier to read and more trustworthy. I kept the formatting the same from chart to chart so the viewer didn’t have to relearn how to read each one. I also made all the bars pink so the visuals matched each other and matched my portfolio design. It sounds small, but it helped the whole page feel like one project instead of a bunch of random charts.

Along with the bar charts, I added a filled map showing fatal collisions per billion miles. I included this because I didn’t want the top 10 charts to feel like they weren’t connected to each state. The map gives a quick overview of how fatal collisions are distributed across the country, which helps frame the bar charts. It also made me think about how ranking-based charts can be useful, but they don’t always show the full picture unless you give the viewer a broader view as well.

Before I made the charts, I expected the states to overlap more. I assumed that states that were high in one risk category would also be high in another. Once I built the visuals, I quickly realized that the outcomes were not what I thought would happen. The top 10 lists changed a lot depending on the factor being measured. Hawaii was one of the few examples where it showed up high in multiple categories, but overall the lists were much more mixed. That was a big takeaway for me because it showed that high risk isn’t necessarily consistent, but instead it depends on what behavior you’re measuring. It also made me realize why it’s important to be specific when you describe patterns, because the story changes depending on which variable you focus on.

The most surprising part of the data for me was how high some of the positive measures were. In the not distracted chart and the no previous accident history chart, the top values were extremely high, basically 96% to 100%. That immediately made me stop and question, because if these are fatal crashes, why are so many drivers recorded as not distracted, or as having no previous accidents? Seeing 100% values for places like the District of Columbia and Mississippi also made me wonder about sample size and how the data was collected. It doesn’t necessarily mean the data is wrong, but it does make me wonder what they are counting as distracted, how previous accident history is recorded, and whether smaller places might have percentages that look extreme because the number of cases is smaller.

Going off of that, this lab also made me think more about definitions. A lot of the questions I ended up with were about what the categories actually mean and whether states measure them the same way. What counts as speeding? What counts as alcohol-impaired? What gets recorded as distracted? I wonder if those definitions are consistent across all states, or if there are differences in how things get reported.

Lab 4 showed me that making a good visualization starts at picking the right dataset and making sure it is clean and that it can portray a story through visualizations. It also showed me that design choices like filtering, formatting, and consistent styling can completely change how readable the final product is. I really like how Tableau makes it easy to experiment with different datasets, and it helped me move quickly from raw data to something visual.
