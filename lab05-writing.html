Lab 5 Reflection

In Lab 5, I used AI assistance to vibe code and add new features to my portfolio. My goal with this lab was to use AI as a support tool, see what it could produce, and then take that output and turn it into something that actually works on my site. I used the AI chat inside VS Code to generate starter code, then I tested everything, adjusted it to fit my site, and added comments showing what I understood and what I still don’t.

My main goal for my portfolio was to make the website easily usable for viewers. I wanted my website to feel more interactive and more like a real public-facing portfolio instead of random static pages. I added three features with the AI assistance. The first feature was click-to-enlarge thumbnails for my images. Most of my images can now be clicked to open a larger version, and there are multiple ways to exit out of it. This made my portfolio feel cleaner and easier to read, because I could keep images smaller on the page without forcing the viewer to squint or scroll through screenshots. The second feature was a hamburger menu, which makes the navigation work better on smaller screens. It opens when the icon is clicked, and it switches to an X so the user can close it easily. The third feature was a scroll-to-top arrow that appears when scrolling further down on the page. Once someone scrolls down, the arrow shows up in the bottom corner, and clicking it smoothly brings them back to the top. All three are small changes, but I think that they make the site feel more polished and more user-friendly.

The biggest thing this lab taught me is how fast AI can help build things, but also how easy it is to end up with code that works even if I don’t fully understand it. When I looked at the code, I could follow the general logic and explain the outcome at a pretty surface level, but I couldn’t have written any of it from scratch on my own. I used the understanding spectrum and added honest comments. I understand about half of the code in terms of how the pieces work together, but there are still parts where I only understand what it does because I tested it and saw the result, not because I could recreate the logic. This lab pushed me to slow down and actually read what was happening line by line instead of just trusting the AI output.

Troubleshooting also became a bigger part of this lab than I expected. For example, my headshot image on the home page, which still does not enlarge like the rest of my thumbnails. I compared that image to the others, tried to research it, and even asked the AI for help, but I still couldn’t fully solve it. That was frustrating, but it also was the most realistic part of the assignment. It showed me that tiny details can break a feature, and even when the code looks almost identical, one small difference can change the outcome. It also reminded me that getting stuck is normal, and the important part is being able to explain what I tried and what I learned from the process.

This lab also helped remind me how important it is to be transparent when using AI. When AI helped me write code, I made it obvious in my portfolio, and stated what I understood and what I didn’t. The goal for me is not to let AI do all of the coding for me, but to use it to learn more and expand what I can build while I’m still developing my skills. AI is helpful for a starting point and adding new features, but it’s only helpful to me if I take the time to learn from it. This lab demonstrated that I can improve my website by adding features that make it more functional and easier to use. I can also use AI to support and enhance my work while still being honest about my understanding.
