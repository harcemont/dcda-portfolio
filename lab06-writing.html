Lab 6 Reflection

In Lab 6, I created my hometown map and embedded it into my portfolio page. I wanted this map to feel personalized instead of looking like a standard default map, so I treated the design as part of the story. My basemap was inspired by the Barbie movie aesthetic because I like the bright, playful look, and I already use pink throughout my portfolio. I found a Barbie inspired color palette and used it to style the map so it felt fun and cohesive instead of realistic and boring. I think that choice matters because the way a map looks affects how someone feels while using it, and I wanted my map to match my personality and fit the rest of my website.

Once I started styling the basemap, I realized pretty quickly that map design is not just picking colors. The biggest cartographic challenge was figuring out how to keep the style while still keeping the map functional. When I zoomed out, there were too many features showing at once, and it looked really cluttered and overwhelming. It distracted from the aesthetic, and it also made it harder to understand what I was even looking at. I had to think about what should be visible at different zoom levels. I decided that when the map is zoomed out, it should mainly highlight the big visual elements like land and water. Then, as someone zooms in, more details like roads and smaller cities can appear. Making that choice helped the map feel cleaner and less overwhelming, but still useful once you zoom in.

Line weight and text were also bigger issues than I expected. Even when the colors looked right, thick lines and bold labels made the map feel too intense and crowded. I ended up making the line weights thinner so the map looked softer and more aesthetic. I also noticed how much the size, thickness, and color of text change readability. If the text blends into the background or feels too heavy, it can ruin the whole look and make the map harder to use. This lab made me realize that small design details can completely change how professional and readable a map feels.

Terrain was another challenge. I wanted some kind of terrain layer because it adds depth and helps the map feel more real, but I could not find a terrain visualization layer in the normal layers or components area. The only terrain option I found was in the global section as 3D terrain. I enabled it so I could get some kind of terrain effect, but I was honestly not fully sure if that was the same thing as a hillshade or contour layer. That forced me to make a judgment call and then document it as part of my process. That was actually a good learning moment because it reminded me that sometimes there is not a perfect option and the important part is choosing the best solution you can, being honest about it, and making sure it supports the goal of the project.

The hardest part of this lab was building and troubleshooting my CSV file and Python script. I ran into a lot of issues with my CSV formatting, and it took me a while to figure out what was wrong. Once I fixed that, I ran into another issue at the end when I previewed my final map and saw weird symbols showing up in the text. That is what made me realize I could not use apostrophes in contractions because it was causing an encoding problem. I went back through and changed the text to remove apostrophes, and that fixed it. That whole process taught me that formatting and encoding issues can break a project even when everything else is correct. It also taught me that debugging is not just about code, it is also about data cleaning and being careful with how information is stored.

I used ChatGPT throughout this lab to help me write parts of the code, debug issues, and fix my CSV so it could be read correctly. It also helped me add a personal touch like using heart markers instead of the typical map pin icon. Lab 6 demonstrates my ability to combine design and technical work in one project. I created a map that feels personal and matches the style of my portfolio, while also making real cartographic choices to reduce clutter and make the map readable at different zoom levels. It also shows that I can work through real technical problems like CSV formatting and encoding issues without giving up. The biggest takeaway for me is that making a map is a mix of creativity, structure, and problem-solving. It is not just about making something look nice, it is about making something that works and communicates clearly while still reflecting the person who made it.
